About the lab

Dr. Rashidatu Abdulazeez oversees the operations of the laboratory, which is affiliated with Droso4Nigeria and DrosAfrica, and situated within the esteemed Department of Zoology at Ahmadu Bello University in Zaria. This facility offers laboratory spaces to Drosophila researchers throughout Nigeria, along with the provision of fly stocks. Driven by a commitment to advancing Drosophila research in Africa, the lab extends its expertise through consultancy and advisory services, guiding others in the establishment, handling, genetic crosses, and execution of Drosophila-related studies.

In addition to its core functions, the lab is dedicated to educational initiatives, conducting training programs and outreach activities in secondary schools throughout Nigeria. For further details on Droso4Nigeria activities, please visit www.droso4nigeria.wordpress.com

Research interest

My interest lies majorly in understanding the effects of ecological stressors on Drosophila population dynamics and species evolution by answering the following questions:

What impact does DAT (Drugs, additives, and toxins) have on a biological organism?

How does DAT shape the neuroanatomy and behavior of an organism?

How does ecological stressors affect the population of an organism?

Does DAT play any role in host-parasite interaction?

To what extent does such effect contribute to evolution of species?

To gain comprehensive insights into the intricacies of biological systems and their responses to various challenges, I explore the neuroanatomy and behavior of the fruit fly, which provides me with a unique perspective on understanding the adaptability and resilience of organisms in response to environmental stressors. In addition, I collaborate in drug screening initiatives to investigate the efficacy and potential applications of various compounds with the goal of identifying substances that could have therapeutic implications or play a role in mitigating the impact of stressors on Drosophila populations

Additionally, my interests extend to exploring host-parasite interactions. I aim to unravel the complexities of relationships between hosts and parasites, investigating the roles (if any) of DAT in such interactions at the ecological and molecular levels.
